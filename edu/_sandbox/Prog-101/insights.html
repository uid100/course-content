Part 1. A Teaching Experience

Internet Protocols

Student Learning Objectives

✔  Describe TCP/IP Packet Transmission

 

Background

Transmission Control Protocol or "TCP/IP" is a common way to transmit data, images, email messages, or other data files, across a computer network. These messages may be divided into smaller packages, or packets and they may take different routes to get from point to point and may arrive at different times and must be reassembled at their destination.

Each numbered packet is sent with a packet header indicating the sender, the intended recipient, the packet number and the expected number of packets.

Preparation

I purchased three small puzzles. Each puzzle was 100 pieces, large enough to take a little bit of time, but small enough to be reasonably assembled in the time that we had in class for the demonstration.

The night before class, I had my kids assemble two of the three puzzles and then carefully turn them over and number each piece in order from top-left to bottom-right and then the puzzles were disassembled and each put in a separate clear plastic bag and I removed one or two pieces at random from each bag.

In the two bags with the numbered puzzle pieces I added instructions to turn the puzzle pieces upside down and assemble by number and to not say a word.

Demonstration

I divided the (competitive) class into three groups and distributed the bags of puzzle pieces and asked them to wait. We talked about what they knew about the puzzle pieces.

They shared that without the box or a picture of the completed puzzle, they wouldn't know what the "message" was until it was assembled. That also meant they wouldn't know if there were missing piecs until they finished building what they had, but decided maybe they could if they counted them, but wouldn't know for sure.

On "go!" I started a timer for effect and to urge their competitive spirit.

They opened the bags and the two groups at the back of the class exchanged knowing looks when they read the instructions and quickly assembled the pieces and each announced (one after another) that they had finished and not only that there were pieces missing, but how many and which ones.

Wrap-up

After the confused group at the front of the class realized what had happened, the students made the connection back to packet transmission and led the discussion, with a few leading questions from me.

They learned that the recipient could send a message receipt back to the sender not only that the message was received, but also how many packets, and which ones were missing, limiting the need to retransmit only those packets that had been lost. And after confirmation, they could be assured that they really were seeing the entire message.

Thoughts

When lecturing, we rely on methods more than repetition to ensure what is sent is also received (and understood.)

In the example, the class was small enough that a lecture might have sufficed.

Going a step further, engaging students in dialog and asking leading questions may have kept more students involved and participating, but this comes along with it's own challenges, time spent when students weren't listening or weren't prepared, asking the next student and the next and fielding unrelated or irrelevant questions and keeping the dialog on topic and on time.

In this case, I also had the benefit of being in a classroom and having a semi-captive audience. Although I have had success with group challenges using virtual breakout-rooms, this example worked because of the shared space.

Observations

Hands-on all students were involved. None were checking emails, working on homework for this or other classes. None were texting or otherwise distracted. They all were driven to win, and all could relate to building a simple puzzle.

There were some brief notes left on the whiteboard from the (very short) discussion before the exercise, which served as reminders and cues to steer the students' conclusions.

Application and Lessons Learned

This was effective because I was confident in the topic and found a way to relate it to something they could easily understand. Students were (regularly) responsive to the energy and excitement that I brought into the classroom. Even over video technology, I can usually gauge understanding by visual and verbal queues.

Moving from the classroom to online means being more creative in keeping students engaged with the instructor and also with other students, but moving to an asynchronous model sometimes proves even more challenging.

In the example above, all students were present and I had instant feedback from their reactions to see that they were actively participating. It was rewarding to find that they were able to articulate all of the planned learning points.

As all of the classes I am currently teaching are asynchronous, my biggest challenge is connection.

  • I spend time preparing curriculum that I hope is visually interesting and easy to follow.
  • I provide very detailed and individual feedback on assignments, focusing at least as much time and effort with top performers as with those struggling with the material.
  • I send weekly announcements sharing questions I've received, and sometimes including personal notes/ "Traveling this week, here are pictures."
  • Using discussion boards. The first is mandatory, and I always include the first post myself to set the tone. Another discussion board is for student discussions about exercises. I participate, but usually just to ask questions.
  • My weekly office hours are an open-forum and are often well-attended. These are not prepared lessons, rather student-led discussions and Q&A sessions. And I often mention them in my announcements, which brings more student interest.
  • My assignments all have an unofficial 24-hour grace period. I often work on projects after midnight so I don't penalize students who do also. In the morning after the 1159PM due date, I set all missing assignments to 0 points and use Canvas to 'send message to students who...' haven't submitted assignments. The 0 points and the email reminders are usually enough to motivate students to finish, and I find I don't have to chase students or deal with frequent issues of turning in late work or missed assignments.

Connection. This is the greatest obstacle, and these are my approaches to closing the gap.
