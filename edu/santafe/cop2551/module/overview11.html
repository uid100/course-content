Properties

Properties are a key feature of C# that provide a flexible mechanism to read, write, or compute the values of private fields. They enable data encapsulation and can include logic in their accessors.

Did we skip some chapters?

As a matter of fact, yes.

All textbook authors divide the content into smaller digestible pieces and sequence them in a way that makes sense and follows a logical progression. This textbook contains a lot of really good content that we will not have time to cover in this course.

The objectives of the course are to (first) provide you with a solid foundation in object-oriented programming, and (second) to introduce you to another popular programming language to practice those patterns and concepts. The additional content in the textbook is worthwhile, and you are encouraged to use this as a good reference for future use.

As much as I do like and appreciate the author's approach and perspective, I believe that an understanding of how and why to use properties is too important a concept to overlook, and I wish the author had prioritized it to include it in earlier chapters. Properties are a fundamental part of C# and are widely used in OOP (and C#) programming.

In our introduction to classes and objects, we said that classes describe entities with attibutes (fields) and behaviors (methods). Properties really fall into a category in between those two and share a lot of the same characteristics as both fields and methods.

Like the author, my approach is to introduce you to the language and the concepts in a way that builds on your existing knowledge and to emphasize what I believe are the most important concepts to learn first.

As the calendar runs out and we approach the end of the time available for this course, I believe that properties are too important to overlook.

Try this!

The Visual Studio editor provides a lot of features that you may not have discovered yet. One of these is the ability to generate properties automatically based on field definitions.

If you have a field defined in your class, you can right-click on the field name and select Quick Actions and Refactorings (or press Ctrl + .). From the menu that appears, select Encapsulate Field. This will generate a property for that field with get and set accessors.

To create a new property, you can also type the prop snippet in the editor and press Tab twice. This will generate a property with a backing field and the get and set accessors.

On a related topic, the author refers to getters and setters as get and set accessors. This is a more formal term that is used, but most developers will refer to them as simply getters and setters. Some authors will also refer to them as accessors and mutators, but this is less common. For common usage, if you use the terms getters and setters, you will be understood by most developers.
